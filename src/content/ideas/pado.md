Most of us love the sea but get to see it perhaps once a year, on a holiday, before going back to our desks where the closest thing to a wave is the water we drink.
So I gave myself a small design brief: what if an ordinary object could hand you a tiny piece of the shoreline, every single day? Not something as static as a photo of the sea but something you invoke and experience.

![Busan Series](/images/ideas/pado/hero.jpg)

Thus, came PADO (*pado* is "wave" in Korean): an insulated tumbler that makes the sound of waves when you tilt it to drink.

### First problem: How do we produce the sound?

I initially flirted with an idea of a straw that would whistle as you sipped, but that meant restricting the idea to a straw-only product. The better solution was to use the one motion every tumbler already demands: **tilting it to drink**. Drinking is the trigger. No button, no app, nothing to remember.

The mechanism is quite simple, borrowed from the ocean drum. Tiny beads roll over curved ridges and create a soft sound of waves crashing on the shore. While the mechanism was simple, implementing it required some more brainstorming and the "skilled" art of compromises.

![The mechanism — beads roll over ridges to create wave sounds](/images/ideas/pado/mechanism.jpg)

### Second: Where does the sound actually live?

My first instinct was to hide the beads inside the tumbler's double wall. Elegant. Invisible. But ultimately wrong. There were two reasons for this.

1. **Acoustic Insulation:** The vacuum gap that keeps your coffee hot is also a brilliant sound insulator, which means the sound itself would be silenced.
2. **Manufacturing Heat:** The beads were meant to be ocean-bound recycled plastic, and vacuum-sealing a steel tumbler involves heat that would turn them into a sad little puddle.

The fix I initially thought of was adding a third layer with the beads around the tumbler, but that meant increasing costs significantly—and what is the use of a bottle that reminds you of the ocean when you could visit the ocean at the same cost?

An hour of racking my brain finally yielded the annoyingly obvious solution: moving the sound out of the wall and into a **separate base module**, snapped on after all the hot manufacturing is done. The cup stays a normal vacuum tumbler. The sound gets its own little component.

#### Tradeoffs:
The honest trade-off is that a timid sip would be quiet, which could actually be a feature for shared office spaces that demand silence.

### Membrane or molded?

Another issue was deciding between using a membrane for the base or keeping it molded. A real ocean drum sounds rich because of a thin, tensioned drumhead (a membrane). Since there is a separate sound module, a membrane could have been built into it. It would sound lovely, but it would also be fragile, finicky, and expensive. It meant compromising on core usability.

The alternative—molding the ridges straight into the base—is cheaper, tougher, and admittedly a bit more "shuffle" than "swell", but highly usable while capturing the unique core experience. Trial and error and some experimentation with materials could improve this further.

### The Fun Part: Moods of the Ocean

The last decision was the most fun. A single design is monotonous and doesn't reflect the "moods" of the ocean and its diversity. 

So PADO isn't just one cup: it's a **(coast)line you can collect**.

*   **A signature horizon:** (sky &rarr; sea &rarr; sand).
*   **A Busan series:** wearing the Gwangan Bridge at night.
*   **World beaches:** white sand, black sand, the eerie glow of a bioluminescent shore.
*   **Seasons of the sea:** one shoreline from dawn to dark.
*   **Kids' series:** friendly, big-eyed sea creatures.

### Closing the Loop

The material closes the loop: a recycled-steel body, with beads made from ocean-bound plastic. The sound of the ocean, made from what we pull out of the ocean.

I started with a brief and ended with a cup that, when you tip it back, hands you a half-second of surf and the sea pretending to be in the room. It won't fool a marine biologist. But in the city traffic, in the sweltering heat, it doesn't have to. It just has to remind you the ocean is still out there, and still worth missing and definitely worth protecting.
